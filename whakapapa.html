Seema Investigates: Is Whakapapa, New Zealand As Funny In Person As On A Map?

Whakapapa — a ski village on Mt Ruapehu, North Island — means "to place in layers" in Māori, which is beautiful. Seema went anyway, skied badly, fell four times, learned something important about the Māori language from a ski instructor named Dave, and won $50 from Paul. She is keeping the $50.
📰
Seema Krishnamurthy
Senior Anchor & Only Adult In The Room  |  Filed from Whakapapa Village, New Zealand 🇳🇿  |  June 3, 2025  |  9 min read
🌋
Mt Ruapehu, Whakapapa ski area. Seema Krishnamurthy is not visible because she had just fallen for the second time. Photo: S. Krishnamurthy / AlphaBro News (self-timer, set before fall)
The Whakapapa ski area on Mt Ruapehu, North Island, New Zealand. The mountain is a stratovolcano. The skiing is excellent. Seema's skiing was not excellent, by her own assessment.  |  AlphaBro News

I should explain how this happened, because the explanation is slightly humiliating and I believe in full journalistic transparency.

Paul and I were looking at a map — a real paper map, which Paul insists on for "authenticity" and which I suspect is actually because he cannot read digital maps — when he pointed at New Zealand and said "Whakapapa." He said it loudly and with evident delight, and then said, "Seema, I bet you fifty dollars you won't go." I said that was not how journalism worked. He said fifty dollars. I booked the flight that evening.

I am not above taking fifty dollars from Paul. I want this on the record. I am a Northwestern-trained journalist with a genuine commitment to rigorous reporting, and I am also not above fifty dollars.

The Name

Whakapapa (pronounced, I should note, approximately "fah-ka-pah-pah," with a soft wh-sound that is specific to Māori and not at all what Paul assumed when he pointed at the map) is a Māori word meaning "to place in layers" — specifically, it refers to genealogy, the act of laying one generation upon another, the way that identity is constructed from the layering of ancestors. It is a word that carries genuine weight in Māori culture, describing something essential about how communities understand their continuity and their place in the world.

It is, in this context, a beautiful name for a place. It is a name that says: this is a location built from layers, from history, from the accumulation of time on a volcanic mountain that is itself layers upon layers of rock and ash and snow.

It does not sound the way Paul thought it sounded when he pointed at the map. I told him this when I called from the airport. He said "sure sure" in the tone of a man who has not changed his position but knows he's been argued at.

The Mountain

Mt Ruapehu is a stratovolcano. It last erupted seriously in 1995-1996. It has a crater lake at its summit that occasionally releases volcanic debris. The ski fields sit on its upper slopes, and the skiing in winter is legitimate alpine skiing on real snow on a real volcano, which is a framing that I found both exciting and, upon reflection, slightly concerning.

I was informed by my ski instructor, a man named Dave who had been teaching skiing on Ruapehu for eleven years, that the volcano was "monitored continuously" and that the ski area had "very good protocols." Dave said this in the calm, competent tone of a man who finds the question reasonable but also slightly amusing. I took it as reassuring. I skied on the volcano.

The Skiing

I want to be honest about my skiing. I am not a good skier. I am a person who has skied a small number of times and has each time achieved a state of competent progression that does not carry over to the next trip. Every time I ski, I am re-learning skiing. I am, always, a beginner.

Dave recognized this immediately. He was patient in a way that suggested eleven years of recognizing exactly what kind of skier he was dealing with within thirty seconds of the chairlift. He did not say "you're not a strong skier." He said "let's start on a green run and get your legs back," which is the polite version of the same information.

I fell four times. The first fall was on the green run. Dave did not comment. The second and third falls were on a blue run I had been feeling confident about, which was a miscalculation. The fourth fall was on the green run again, at the end of the day, which I attribute to fatigue and which Dave attributed, diplomatically, to "muscle memory taking the afternoon off."

I was cold, I was tired, and I had fallen four times, and Mt Ruapehu was a volcano, and the other skiers on the mountain were all visibly better than me, and I had flown to New Zealand for fifty dollars, and it was, in spite of all of this, an excellent day.

The Dave Explanation

At lunch — a café at the ski lodge, standard ski lodge food, 7.5, acceptable — I asked Dave about the Māori meaning of Whakapapa. He had clearly answered this question before and seemed glad to answer it again. He explained the genealogical meaning, the concept of identity through layering, the significance of the name in the context of the mountain, which is a place of cultural importance to the iwi of the region, the Ngāti Tūwharetoa, who have connections to this land going back many generations.

"So it's actually a very serious name," I said.

"Yes," Dave said.

"And most people who come here because of the name are coming because they think it sounds like something else."

Dave looked at his soup for a moment. "Yes," he said. "That's pretty much it."

"And you've been having this exact conversation for eleven years."

"The job has many dimensions," Dave said, and ate his soup, and I liked him very much.

Verdict

Is Whakapapa, New Zealand as funny in person as on a map? It is not, because it is actually a beautiful word with a meaningful and culturally significant Māori etymology, and the mountain it names is a genuine and excellent ski destination that deserves to be visited on its own considerable merits. The skiing is real, the mountain is spectacular, Dave is an excellent instructor, and the name means something important.

Come for the skiing. Not the name. The name is better than the joke you had in mind. This is also true of Dildo, in a different way. This is, I am starting to think, true of most places.

I am keeping the fifty dollars.

— Seema Krishnamurthy, filed from Whakapapa Village. Dave: thank you for the patience. The fourth fall was my fault.
