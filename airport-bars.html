Paul's Mambo Taxi World Tour: Rating 38 Airport Bars Across Four Continents

Paul Largent has visited 38 airport bars on four continents, ordered 38 cocktails, and produced a definitive ranked list. Nairobi's Terminal 2 leads the field. A US airport he refuses to name is last. The methodology is explained. The peanuts at Nairobi are part of the methodology.
🕵️‍♂️
Paul "Mambo Taxi" Largent
Lead Anchor & Chief Investigative Correspondent  |  🌍 Various Airports  |  June 3, 2025  |  12 min read
🍺

Terminal 2, Jomo Kenyatta International Airport, Nairobi, Kenya. Ranked #1 in Paul Largent's definitive airport bar survey. Score: 9.2. "The peanuts alone justify the trip to Nairobi," Largent reports.  |  AlphaBro News

I have been doing this for three years. I want that on the record. Three years of deliberate, systematic, peer-reviewed — Seema did not review any of this, but the principle holds — airport bar research, conducted in the service of a simple question: where, in the world's airports, can a person get a genuinely good drink in a genuinely good environment, without being charged twenty dollars for the privilege?

The answer is: mostly not in America. But we'll get there.

The Methodology

Each bar was rated on five criteria:

  • Cocktail quality — was it well-made? Was the ice appropriate? Did they know what they were doing?
  • Speed of service — you are in an airport. The gate could be called. This matters.
  • Natural light — airport bars are often underground bunkers. Those with windows are measurably better for the soul.
  • Bar snacks — quality of snacks provided. Peanuts are rated separately. The Nairobi peanuts changed my life.
  • Vibe of a man who could disappear here for 3 hours — this is the most important criterion. Does this bar create the conditions under which a person might, reasonably, lose track of time? A good airport bar should feel like a destination, not a waiting room.

The Top Five

#1: Jomo Kenyatta International, Nairobi — Terminal 2 bar — 9.2

I am going to talk about the peanuts. The peanuts at this bar are served in a small clay bowl, warm, salted with something that is not regular salt, seasoned with — I asked; they told me; I have attempted to replicate it at home and failed — a blend that includes chili and lime and something I couldn't identify that I have since described to seven different chefs, none of whom could tell me what it was. The cocktail was a gin and tonic made with a local gin that I also cannot identify but have thought about several times since. The bar has large windows overlooking the tarmac. The staff knew I was going to miss the general vibe of urgency you get at most airports and they seemed, collectively, fine with this. I stayed two and a half hours. My flight was delayed, so this was technically appropriate. I have returned to this bar twice since. The peanuts have been the same both times. This is the mark of a serious operation.

#2: Tokyo Narita — Gate departure lounge bar — 8.9

The precision. The absolute precision of this operation. My Suntory highball arrived in exactly the correct glass, with exactly the correct amount of ice, in approximately four minutes from ordering, which in airport terms approaches the miraculous. The bar snacks were a small wooden board of edamame and pickled vegetables. Everything was clean. The bar top was clean. The bartender's uniform was clean in a way that made me self-conscious about my own travel clothing choices. The vibe was: a person who respects you enough to make your drink correctly. I respected them back. 8.9.

#3: Bangkok Suvarnabhumi — international departures — 8.5

At 6am, this bar was full of people with very interesting stories. I know this because I was there at 6am, having caught a pre-dawn flight from somewhere I don't fully remember, and the man to my left was a contractor returning from Riyadh and the woman to my right was a marine biologist who had been in the Gulf of Thailand for three weeks. I ordered a Singha and a bowl of peanuts and listened to both of them for forty-five minutes and did not say much because I didn't need to. The bar gave me that. That's a 9 for vibe right there. The cocktail dropped it slightly — it was fine but not distinctive. 8.5 overall, but that's underselling the experience.

#4: Changi Airport, Singapore — Terminal 3 rooftop — 8.3

Singapore Changi is the best airport in the world, which means its bar has extremely high competition from the rest of the airport. The orchid garden is outside. There is a butterfly house. Why would you sit in a bar? You would sit in a bar because you've already done the butterfly house (I've done it three times; it remains excellent) and you want a cocktail before your flight, and this bar delivers one competently, with natural light and a good view. 8.3.

#5: Cape Town International — domestic arrivals bar — 8.1

Technically I was in arrivals, not departures, which is not how most people use airport bars. But I had a six-hour layover and the arrivals bar had a Pinotage on the list that was, at 11am in an airport, a 9.0 glass of wine. The overall bar was 8.1. The Pinotage was a personal discovery that I have since pursued across four South African wine regions. I have not found a better glass in an airport since. I have found better glasses in actual vineyards. This still counts.

The Bottom Three

Last place: An American airport I will not name — 2.1

I am not naming it. It is in a major US city and the bar is near a gate that serves three popular domestic routes and it is full, at every hour of the day, with the specific energy of people who have accepted that this is what air travel is and have decided to be very loud about it. They charged eighteen dollars for a domestic lager. The TV was showing golf with no sound. The bar snacks were a bag of chips in a small wire rack that I was asked to select myself. The bartender was handling six people simultaneously through no fault of his own. It took twenty-two minutes to get my beer. It cost eighteen dollars. I drank it in nine minutes. 2.1.

I want to explain the 2.1 rather than a lower score: the beer was cold. The bartender, despite everything, was pleasant. The chairs were comfortable. This is the ceiling that American airport bars have built for themselves, and I want to acknowledge that the ceiling is occasionally met.

The Overall Theory

Here is what I have concluded, after three years and 38 airport bars across four continents: the best airport bars are in places where people do not stress about missing their flight. Nairobi, Tokyo, Bangkok — these are airports where the culture of the bar exists independently of the anxiety of the airport. The bar is not a waiting room. It is a room. You are in it. Time applies differently here.

American airport bars, with notable exceptions, have not managed this. They are waiting rooms that serve alcohol. There is a difference, and the difference is the peanuts in a clay bowl at Nairobi that someone seasoned with something I still can't identify.

I will keep looking. The spreadsheet is at 38. I want it to be 50. There are airports I haven't been to yet. They have bars. I have opinions about those bars already, which is the wrong approach methodologically, but is also, I think, what journalism requires: you have to care about the peanuts before you know they're going to be good.

— Paul "Mambo Taxi" Largent. The full 38-bar ranked list is available upon request. It is forty-seven pages. Seema has asked me not to publish all forty-seven pages. I am respecting this, for now.
