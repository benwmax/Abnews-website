Intercourse, PA: Paul Drives Four Hours And Finds Mostly Amish People

In a necessary journalistic pilgrimage to Intercourse, Pennsylvania (population: 1,334), Paul Largent found a quilt shop, a pretzel bakery, a jam store, several Amish buggies, and zero bars. He gave the town sign a 7.2. He ate a pretzel.
🕵️‍♂️
Paul "Mambo Taxi" Largent
Lead Anchor & Chief Investigative Correspondent  |  Filed from Intercourse, PA 🇺🇸  |  June 1, 2025  |  9 min read
🐄

The Intercourse, Pennsylvania town sign. Population 1,334. Largent photographed it twenty-three times, which he notes is "significantly fewer than Clit, but Clit set an unreasonably high bar."  |  AlphaBro News

I drove four hours from my apartment in Chicago to Intercourse, Pennsylvania, and I want to say upfront: I drove four hours. That is a factually accurate statement. Four hours. In a rental car that smelled faintly of someone else's ambitions. Through Indiana and Ohio and into the green hills of Lancaster County. Four hours, to a place called Intercourse, because that is the job.

Seema told me I didn't need to go in person. "You could just write something based on publicly available information," she said. I asked if she had ever heard of immersive journalism. She said she had, and that this wasn't it. I drove to Intercourse anyway.

First Impressions

I arrived at 11am on a Tuesday, which — I was to discover — is an excellent time to be in Intercourse, Pennsylvania, if you enjoy quilts, pretzels, and a general atmosphere of peaceful agricultural commerce. It is not an excellent time if you were hoping for anything else, because there is nothing else.

The town is, genuinely, exactly what it looks like from the outside: a small Amish and Mennonite community in Lancaster County, famous primarily for the name, secondarily for its role in the Old Order Amish cultural tourist economy, and tertiarily — if that's a word, Seema will know — for the jam.

The jam is, I should say, excellent. I bought four jars: peach, strawberry rhubarb, apple butter, and something labeled "Dutch Relish" that I cannot fully classify but have been eating with everything. I was prepared to find the jam dismissible. I found it instead very good. This is the kind of thing that happens to you when you go to Intercourse, Pennsylvania with an open mind. You buy Dutch Relish and mean it.

The Sign

The Intercourse town sign is on the main road, Route 340, and is — I want to be precise about this — a 7.2 on the Mambo Taxi scale. Not lower, because it is a real town sign saying INTERCOURSE in official Pennsylvania typography and that is inherently funny. Not higher, because I have been to Clit, Turkey, and the Clit sign set a standard that Intercourse, with its more clinical spelling, cannot quite match.

I photographed it twenty-three times. At Clit I got to forty-seven. The drop-off reflects no disrespect to Intercourse — it reflects that I have grown, as a photographer and as a man, more economical. Twenty-three photographs is more than enough to document a sign. I know this now. Clit taught me this.

An Amish buggy passed while I was photographing. The horse appeared uninterested. The occupants also appeared uninterested, which suggests they have seen tourists doing this before and have made a collective decision not to acknowledge it. A decision I respect.

The Amish Situation

I should address this directly: I did not successfully interview anyone in Intercourse.

This is not for lack of effort. It is because the Old Order Amish community, which comprises a significant portion of the area's population, generally does not seek media attention and especially does not seek the kind of media attention I represent. My attempt to explain AlphaBro News to a man at the pretzel bakery — a Mennonite gentleman who did speak to me, but briefly — ended when I said "funny journalism" and he looked at me for a moment and then handed me a pretzel and accepted my two dollars and considered the transaction complete.

The pretzel was excellent. Soft, perfectly salted, warm. I gave it an 8.6. I ate two.

The Bar Situation (Critical Update)

There is not a bar in Intercourse. I knew this going in, but knowing something intellectually and experiencing it physically at 3pm after four hours of driving are different things.

I drove to the nearest gas station, which was three miles away, and bought a cold can of Yuengling, which is a Pennsylvania beer and therefore appropriate. I sat in the rental car in the gas station parking lot and drank it and looked at my phone and considered the nature of this work.

Here is what I concluded: place-name tourism is a transaction. You show up, you photograph the sign, you eat the pretzel, you buy four jars of jam you didn't know you needed, you drive home with a story that is, at its core, about the gap between expectation and reality. Intercourse promised a punchline. It delivered a quilt shop and excellent soft pretzels and Amish buggies rolling past in the afternoon light. And honestly? The afternoon light in Lancaster County is genuinely beautiful. Rolling green fields, white farmhouses, no one trying to sell you anything more complicated than jam.

I had expected disappointment. I found, instead, something more interesting: a completely functional community that has no idea why I drove four hours to photograph its sign, and no particular interest in finding out.

They may be onto something.

Intercourse, PA: 6.7 on the Mambo Taxi scale. Points lost for no bar and the gas station Yuengling. Points awarded for the jam (genuinely), the pretzel (definitely), and the horse that looked directly at me while I was photographing the sign, which I am choosing to interpret as a nod of journalistic solidarity.

— Paul "Mambo Taxi" Largent, reporting from the gas station three miles outside Intercourse, Pennsylvania.
