Are French People Actually Rude, Or Is It Just That Seema Doesn't Speak French?

A six-day cultural study in which Krishnamurthy systematically tested the rudeness hypothesis across bakeries, museums, cafés, and a supermarket. Methodology: increasingly confident pointing. Conclusion: "Once I stopped speaking English at people, everyone was extremely nice. Also the croissants are unreal."
📰
Seema Krishnamurthy
Senior Anchor & Only Adult In The Room  |  Filed from Paris, France 🇫🇷  |  May 28, 2025  |  10 min read
🥐
A croissant. The croissant. The one from Boulangerie Martin, 7th arrondissement. Photo: S. Krishnamurthy / AlphaBro News
The croissant from Boulangerie Martin, Rue Saint-Dominique, 7th arrondissement, Paris. Krishnamurthy photographed it before eating it, which she describes as "the most journalism I have done on this trip."  |  AlphaBro News

Let me state my methodology upfront, because I believe transparency is important in cultural research of this kind.

Day one: I spoke English. Directly, confidently, and, in retrospect, somewhat loudly. I received what I have come to term "Paris face" — not hostility, exactly, but a specific kind of patient neutrality that communicates, without words, a range of emotions from mild inconvenience to philosophical resignation. Nobody was rude. Nobody was warm. Transactions were completed. I left each interaction with the distinct impression that I was a problem that had been efficiently solved.

Day two: I stopped speaking English. I switched to pointing and saying s'il vous plaît. Everything changed immediately.

The Bakery (Day Two)

I want to spend time on this because I think it is the central finding of the study.

I walked into a boulangerie on the Rue Saint-Dominique in the 7th arrondissement at 8:15 in the morning. The boulangerie was called Boulangerie Martin. There was a queue of four people. I joined the queue. When I reached the counter, I said "bonjour" — my French begins and ends here, with this one word, delivered with what I hoped was appropriate seriousness — and then I pointed at a croissant in the case and said "s'il vous plaît" and held out a five-euro note.

The woman behind the counter — mid-fifties, wearing an apron, clearly a person who has been in this bakery since approximately 5am and has strong opinions about the correct temperature for butter — smiled at me. A real smile. She wrapped the croissant in tissue paper and said something I didn't understand and handed me my change and said "bonne journée" and I said "bonne journée" back, which is the only other French I know, and I walked out into the Paris morning with a croissant in tissue paper and felt, genuinely, like a person who had done something correctly for the first time in two days.

The croissant.

I need to tell you about the croissant from Boulangerie Martin on the Rue Saint-Dominique in Paris. I have eaten croissants in fourteen countries. I have eaten croissants from airport bakeries and hotel breakfast buffets and patisseries and supermarkets and a gas station in rural Belgium, and I am not a person who uses the word "transcendent" about food because I think it is imprecise and also because Paul uses it about everything and I have developed a reflexive skepticism. But the croissant from Boulangerie Martin was transcendent.

It was laminated correctly — you could see the layers, distinct and golden, each one separate — with a crust that shattered rather than bent and an interior that was, and I am going to say this with full professional awareness that it sounds excessive, like a small warm room you want to move into. The butter was present throughout but not overwhelming. The whole thing was about twelve centimeters long and I ate it standing on the pavement in forty-five seconds and then stood there for another thirty seconds thinking about what had just happened.

I went back the next day. And the day after that. I went four times total. Each time: bonjour, point, s'il vous plaît, bonne journée. Each time: warm, efficient, correct. The croissant was the same each time. This is the boulangerie's achievement: that you can produce that croissant, consistently, at scale, every morning at 8:15, without it declining into ordinariness. I do not know how they do this. I respect it.

The Museum, The Café, The Supermarket

The Musée d'Orsay: I used the pointing method for the audio guide and the café. Both transactions were pleasant. A guard nodded at me near the Renoirs. I nodded back. This is the full extent of my cultural exchange with the Musée d'Orsay and I found it entirely satisfying.

A café on the Left Bank: I ordered an espresso by pointing at the espresso machine and raising one finger. The waiter said something in French. I said "oui" with what I hoped was the confidence of someone who understood what they had agreed to. He brought an espresso and a small glass of water. This is what I wanted. Either he understood me correctly, or the question was whether I wanted water with my espresso, to which the answer was always yes. Either way: successful transaction, excellent espresso, 8.7.

The Monoprix: I bought cheese, wine, and a small pot of mustard by placing them on the counter and handing the cashier my card. She scanned everything, said "quatre-vingt-dix centimes" at some point, and I gave her an extra euro and she gave me change. Total conversation: approximately eleven words, none of them mine. The cashier was perfectly pleasant. The mustard cost ninety cents and is the best mustard I have eaten in my life.

Conclusion

French people are not rude. The evidence of this study is clear and consistent: when approached with a bonjour, a point, and a s'il vous plaît, French people are efficient, helpful, and occasionally warm. The perceived rudeness is, in my assessment, a calibration issue. They do not love being spoken to in English by a journalist who is using them as research subjects. They do not love loud transactions in a language they have not chosen. Once you remove these variables, what remains is a population of people who are simply going about their day and would prefer you to do the same, as quietly and politely as possible, preferably in their language, but a sincere point and a good attitude will largely suffice.

This is not, when I write it out, a surprising conclusion. But I think the croissant is worth documenting regardless.

Four visits to Boulangerie Martin. Four perfect croissants. I have the address. I am keeping it.

— Seema Krishnamurthy, Paris. Paul asked if he could come on the France trip. I said no. He came anyway and ate seventeen things in one afternoon and described all of them as "transcendent." I maintain my methodological distance.
