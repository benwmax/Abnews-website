Paul Expensed A Camel Ride In Egypt "For Research." I Reviewed The Receipt. It Was Two Camels.

Seema Krishnamurthy manages the AlphaBro News expense reports. She has reviewed Paul's Egypt receipts. There are two camel rides on the receipt. Paul says one camel was "for context." Seema has questions. She is filing this piece instead of sending those questions to Paul directly, because she knows how that conversation ends.
📰
Seema Krishnamurthy
Senior Anchor & Only Adult In The Room  |  Opinion  |  June 1, 2025  |  5 min read
🐪
Two camels. The Giza Plateau. One is "for context." According to Paul. Photo: P. Largent / AlphaBro News
The Egypt expense report. Two camel rides. Line item: "Camel, research — x2." Explanation provided: "one for context." Seema's assessment of this explanation: pending.  |  AlphaBro News

I want to begin with the receipt itself, because I believe in primary sources.

The receipt, issued by a camel guide operation at the Giza Plateau, Giza, Egypt, dated three weeks ago, itemizes the following: two camel rides, one hour each, including guide, at a rate that is standard for the area and not, individually, unreasonable. The total was reasonable for one person. For two people it would have been reasonable. But there is one receipt. One person's name is on it. That person is Paul "Mambo Taxi" Largent, and I know, because I was also in Cairo at the time, that Paul Largent was there alone.

Two camels.

One Paul.

I have questions.

The Explanation I Was Given

When I flagged the receipt in our expense tracking system — which is a shared spreadsheet, because we are a publication with a remarkable travel budget and zero administrative infrastructure — Paul responded. He responded quickly, which I note because Paul typically responds to expense queries with a speed inversely proportional to the legitimacy of the expense. Quick response means he has had an answer prepared.

His answer: "the second camel was for context."

I stared at this for a moment. Then I responded: "Context of what?"

Paul: "what it looks like to have two camels."

Me: "That is not what research means."

Paul: "it absolutely is what research means seema come on."

I did not come on. I am still not coming on. I am instead writing this piece, which is the appropriate journalistic response to an expense report that contains the phrase "one for context."

The Cases For And Against

I want to be fair. I always want to be fair. Paul's Egypt trip produced the Pyramids piece, which was assigned as a joke question and became, I think, a genuinely interesting meditation on wonder and scale and the absurdity of asking an absurd question about an extraordinary thing. The piece has a genuine conclusion. I am proud of it in the way I am proud of the cricket piece — not because it is what either of us intended, but because the journalism, when it arrived, was real.

So: the trip was legitimate. The camel ride — one camel ride — was legitimate. A journalist visiting the Giza Plateau for research purposes has a reasonable claim for one camel ride as an immersive experience. I would approve this. I did approve this, in the sense that I processed the receipt for one camel and flagged the second camel as requiring explanation.

The explanation was "for context."

The second camel, per Paul's subsequent clarification, was not ridden by another person. Paul rode one camel. The second camel walked alongside, led by the guide. Paul describes this as "seeing what it looks like from the outside, which you can only do if there's a second camel to look at." I have been a journalist for eight years and this is not a research methodology that appears in any framework I encountered at Northwestern. I have checked. It is not there.

What I Have Decided

I have approved the expense, with a note in the system that reads: "Camel 1: approved. Camel 2: approved under protest, category amended to 'context.'" I have added "context" as a line item in our budget categories. It now sits between "accommodation" and "ground transport" in the spreadsheet. Paul found this funny. I found it necessary.

This is the texture of managing the AlphaBro News finances. The receipts are from forty-seven countries. They include: a hospital in Bangkok (approved), twelve t-shirts that say something about Austria (disputed, then approved), four jars of jam from Lancaster County, Pennsylvania (approved; the Dutch Relish does sound genuinely good), and now two camels at the Giza Plateau, one of which was for context.

I went to Medill. I did eighteen thousand words on verification methodology in conflict zones. I am approving camel receipts under a budget line called "context."

I am fine. I said that in the last piece too. The position has not changed.

But the camel was for context. I want that on the record. That is what he said. Context. One hour. Giza Plateau. Not ridden. Present. For context.

The pyramids piece was good. I am still approving the camel under protest. Both of these things are true at the same time.

— Seema Krishnamurthy, Senior Anchor and de facto Finance Manager, AlphaBro News. Paul has been informed this piece is being published. He responded: "nice." He does not have veto power. He knows this.
