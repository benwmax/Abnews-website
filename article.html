Paul Largent Travels 6,200 Miles To Find Clit, Turkey — And Sends Back 47 Photos Of The Welcome Sign

🕵️‍♂️
Paul "Mambo Taxi" Largent

Filed from Clit, Iğdır Province, Eastern Turkey 🇹🇷  |  Published June 4, 2025  |  8 min read

It is a Wednesday afternoon in eastern Turkey, 97 degrees Fahrenheit, and I am standing in front of a sign that says CLIT in large white letters on a blue background. I have never been happier.

Let me back up.

The village of Clit (population: approximately 1,500; local pronunciation: "Chliyt", which is less funny but I respect it) sits in the Iğdır Province of northeastern Turkey, hard against the Iranian border and roughly 6,200 miles from my apartment in Chicago. I have been trying to get here for two years.

It started, as all great journalism does, with a bar bet. My colleague Seema told me the village existed. I said it did not. She said it did. I said I'd fly there and see for myself. She said that was not how fact-checking worked. I booked the flight anyway.

Getting There

The journey to Clit is not easy. From Chicago, you fly to Istanbul (eleven hours), then to Erzurum (two more hours), then rent a car that the agency describes as "reliable" in a tone that suggests they are praying, and drive east for approximately three hours on a road that starts confident and gradually becomes more of a suggestion.

I made this trip in late May, which the guidebooks describe as "ideal." The guidebooks have not been to this part of Turkey in late May. It was fine. The pastry at a roadside stop outside Iğdır city was outstanding. I am giving it a 9.1 on the Mambo Taxi scale, which I have adapted for food.

I arrived at dusk on a Tuesday, checked into a small guesthouse run by a man named Orhan who, upon hearing I had come specifically to photograph the village sign, sighed in a way that suggested I was not the first person to have done this. I was not. I know because he keeps a notebook.

"You are the fourteenth this year," Orhan told me, pouring tea. "Last month there were two Germans. A Japanese man came in 2022. He was here for four days."

I asked if the journalists ever wrote anything about the actual village.

Orhan looked at me for a long time. "No," he said.

The Sign

I found the sign the next morning. It is on the main road into the village, blue and white, standard Turkish municipal signage, reading CLIT in crisp capital letters with a population figure and elevation. It is completely, aggressively mundane. It is the funniest thing I have ever seen.

I took forty-seven photographs.

This is not hyperbole. Forty-seven photographs: with flash, without flash, from the left, from the right, close up on just the letters, wide shot with the mountains behind, a selfie, a video panning slowly across the letters while I describe it like a nature documentary narrator, and finally a short film in which I approach the sign from a distance as if encountering it for the first time, even though this was the fifteenth time.

Orhan watched from his car for about twenty minutes before driving away.

The Village Itself

Clit is, it must be said, a lovely village. The surrounding landscape — the Aras River valley, the distant bulk of Mount Ararat to the west — is genuinely spectacular. The people were warm and hospitable once they established that I was not a threat, merely an idiot.

I ate lunch at a family home after a series of gestures and my most confident pointing established that yes, I would like food and yes, I would pay for it. I was served lamb, flatbread, and a yogurt dish I could not identify but ate entirely. I paid what seemed like a reasonable amount. The grandmother of the household photographed me back, which felt fair.

The village has a small mosque, a tea house where older men gather in the afternoons, several streets of stone houses, and fields of beet and potato spreading out toward the Iranian border. There is a good hill behind the village with a view of the whole valley. I climbed it. There was no one up there.

The Bar

There is not, technically, a bar in Clit. This was a setback.

I found the closest approximation: a small tea house that also served beer, if you asked for beer specifically and accepted that it would be warm. I accepted this. I had a warm Efes lager and attempted to explain AlphaBro News to the proprietor, a man named Yusuf who spoke no English but seemed entertained by my efforts.

I rated the experience 8.8 on the Mambo Taxi scale, adjusted for the warmth of the beer and the extraordinary context of the setting.

What I Found

I was expecting a punchline. I found a village. A real one, with real people, real history, real lamb that was frankly excellent, and a sign that means nothing to its residents and everything to about fourteen visitors a year who come with cameras and no particular purpose.

The name, for what it's worth, has nothing to do with anything. It derives from an old Turkic root. It just ended up spelled the way it's spelled. The village did not choose this. The village has opinions about it that it keeps largely to itself.

I stayed two nights. On the second night I sat outside the guesthouse with Orhan and his brother and we drank tea and watched the sun go down behind Ararat and I tried to explain why I had come all this way and he nodded patiently and asked if I wanted more tea and I said yes and that was the whole conversation.

It was one of the best nights I've had reporting.

Final Score

Clit, Turkey: 9.4 out of 10 on the Mambo Taxi scale. Lose a point for the warm beer. Gain it back for the lamb. Net zero on the sign because that was my issue, not theirs.

Would I go back? Yes. Will I tell Seema she was right about the fact-checking? Absolutely not. That is not what journalism is for.

— Paul "Mambo Taxi" Largent, Clit, Turkey
