I Have A Master's Degree In Journalism And I Just Spent A Week In A Town Called Dildo. I Am Fine.

Seema Krishnamurthy, Northwestern Medill School of Journalism, 2017, reflects on her career trajectory: a promising start, some good work, and then Paul, and now Dildo. She is philosophical about it. The fish was good.
📰
Seema Krishnamurthy
Senior Anchor & Only Adult In The Room  |  Opinion  |  June 5, 2025  |  5 min read
📰
Seema Krishnamurthy. Northwestern, 2017. This is not how she planned things. Photo: AlphaBro News
Seema Krishnamurthy, Senior Anchor, AlphaBro News. Previously: two staff positions, one freelance contract, one fellowship. Currently: Dildo.  |  AlphaBro News

Let me tell you about my master's thesis.

It was 18,000 words on the evolving role of digital media in covering conflict zones, with particular focus on verification methodology in low-resource reporting environments. My thesis advisor called it "rigorous and genuinely useful." I submitted it in April 2017. I had already accepted a staff position at a publication I was proud to name on my CV. I had a plan. The plan involved serious journalism.

Last week, I spent three days in a town called Dildo, Newfoundland, filed 4,000 words about a fisheries museum, and bought four copies of a book about Trinity Bay cod stocks because they had sold out of everything else.

I am fine. I want to be clear about this: I am fine.

How I Got Here

The career trajectory is, in retrospect, visible as a trajectory only in hindsight. At the time it felt like a series of individual decisions, each of which seemed reasonable on its own terms.

The staff position was good. Then there was a restructuring. Then there was freelancing, which is its own kind of education. Then there was a contact who said, "Have you heard of AlphaBro News? It's a new thing, it's funny, the travel budget is remarkable." And here I am.

The travel budget is, I will say, remarkable. I have been to forty-two countries in two years. I have eaten things I would not have otherwise eaten and seen things I would not have otherwise seen. I have interviewed a camel guide in Egypt and a cricket-explaining retired banker in Mumbai and a ski instructor in New Zealand and a fisheries museum volunteer in Dildo, Newfoundland, and I have filed serious, thorough pieces about all of them.

The pieces are, if I may say, good. The Dildo piece is actually quite good. The cricket piece is, I believe, the best sports writing I have ever produced, which is complicated because I understand roughly 60% of the sport, but I think the 60% shows.

The Paul Question

People ask about Paul. "What is it like working with Paul?" is a question I receive in a tone that implies the questioner suspects the answer is either "impossibly chaotic" or "secretly wonderful." The answer is: mostly the first thing, occasionally the second, and always, always expensive in ways that I am the one processing the receipts for.

Paul sent me to Dildo at 11:47pm on a Tuesday with the message "dildo nfl real town??? investigate???" I booked the flights at 9am Wednesday. This is not my best quality, the immediate responding to Paul's midnight Slacks. But the piece was good, and the fish and chips were outstanding, and the hiking trail that Margaret told me about had a view of Trinity Bay that I have not stopped thinking about.

So. I don't know. It's complicated.

The Verdict On The Career

Here is what I have decided, which I am calling a verdict because it is an opinion piece and that's what you do in opinion pieces:

The journalism is real. The 4,000 words on Dildo are real journalism — they are accurate, they are thorough, they treat the subject with appropriate seriousness, and they contain some of the best fish and chips reporting I am aware of. The cricket piece is 2,800 words of genuine cultural observation about a sport I learned in a stadium over five days from a man named Rajan. The pyramids piece answered an absurd question with more rigor than it deserved and produced, I think, a genuinely interesting philosophical conclusion about monuments and the nature of wonder.

Northwestern trained me to do journalism. AlphaBro News has given me journalism to do, in places and on subjects and in circumstances that no serious publication would have sent me to, and I have done it seriously, and mostly it has been good.

I don't regret my choices, exactly. "Exactly" is doing some work in that sentence, but I meant it when I put it there. The fish in Dildo was genuinely among the best I've eaten. The whale postcard is on my desk. The $50 from Paul is in my wallet. The hiking trail with Trinity Bay below in the morning light — nobody wrote about that before I did.

My thesis advisor would have opinions about all of this. I have decided not to tell him. Some things are better left as private peace.

I am fine. I'll be in Vietnam next week. Paul wants me to "verify his soup scores." I have a feeling about what that means. The flights are already booked.

— Seema Krishnamurthy, Senior Anchor, AlphaBro News. Northwestern Medill, 2017. Filed from an apartment that currently smells like Trinity Bay cod stock books, which is a sentence I did not expect to be able to write at this point in my career.
