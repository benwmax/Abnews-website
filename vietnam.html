Paul Reviews Every Soup He Ate In Vietnam Over 11 Days And Nobody Asked Him To

The man ate 23 bowls of pho in Hanoi alone. He has opinions. He has scores. He has a color-coded spreadsheet. He found bún bò Huế in Hue and considers it "the greatest soup encounter of my life, and I have lived a full life." AlphaBro News has published all of it.
🕵️‍♂️
Paul "Mambo Taxi" Largent
Lead Anchor & Chief Investigative Correspondent  |  Filed from Vietnam 🇻🇳  |  June 1, 2025  |  12 min read
🍵
Bowl #17, Hanoi. 7am. The broth has been going since 3am. Photo: P. Largent / AlphaBro News
Bowl number seventeen of the Hanoi pho survey. 7am, Old Quarter. The broth had been on since 3am, according to the proprietor, who communicated this via gestures and a meaningful look at the stockpot.  |  AlphaBro News

I want to say, before I give you the scores, that this trip changed me. I know that sounds like the kind of thing a person says about a trip to Vietnam when they went for the Instagram content and had a good week. That is not what I mean. I mean that Vietnam's soup culture — the range of it, the depth of it, the way that a bowl of pho at 6am in Hanoi is a completely different cultural object from a bowl of bún bò Huế at noon in Hue, which is itself nothing like the bánh canh at a street stall in Hội An — is more complex and more beautiful than I had any right to expect.

I have been eating food in forty-seven countries for three years. I have a spreadsheet for most of it. Nothing prepared me for Vietnamese soup. Nothing.

I ate twenty-three bowls of pho. I also ate many other things that were not pho, which I will get to. But twenty-three bowls of pho first, because that is where this started.

The Hanoi Pho Survey (23 Bowls, 5 Days)

My methodology for the pho survey was modeled, loosely, on wine tasting: I ordered the same bowl at twenty-three different establishments, I paid attention to what was in it and what was not in it, and I took notes. Unlike wine tasting, I finished every bowl. There is no spitting in soup journalism. That is the rule.

What I found: pho is not a single dish. It is a family of dishes with a common structure — the clear beef broth, the noodles, the toppings — and a range of variation that spans, at the extremes, two entirely different experiences. The pho I ate at a famous Old Quarter institution at 6am (Bowl #7, score: 9.3) was crystalline, precise, the broth achieving a clarity that felt architectural — you could taste each element distinctly, the star anise, the char, the bone. The pho I ate at a small family shop in the Tây Hồ district (Bowl #14, score: 9.1) was richer, darker, the kind of pho that has decided it doesn't need to explain itself.

Both were correct. Neither was wrong. This is a thing I am still thinking about.

The lowest-scoring bowl was a tourist-district shop that gave me brown broth and pre-packaged noodles and charged twice what anyone else charged. Score: 5.2. I ate it anyway. I always eat it anyway. It's the principle.

Hue and the Greatest Soup Encounter of My Life

I need to tell you about the bún bò Huế.

Bún bò Huế is a central Vietnamese soup: a lemongrass-and-shrimp-paste broth, round rice noodles, sliced beef, and pork, typically with a chili oil that builds heat over the course of the bowl in a way that sneaks up on you and then, about two-thirds through, becomes the main event. It is from Hue. The best version of it is in Hue, at a tiny shop on a side street that I found by following a woman carrying her lunch and asking, with gestures, where she'd gotten it.

She showed me. The shop had eight plastic chairs and a woman in her seventies who ran everything with an efficiency that made me feel, as I often do in the presence of genuine mastery, slightly embarrassed on behalf of my entire industry.

The bowl she gave me was a 9.8. I have given three things in my life a score above 9.5: the Nairobi peanuts (9.5 on a later revisit), a sunset in the Maldives that I was professionally obligated to rate and gave a 9.6 as a matter of completeness, and this bowl of bún bò Huế in Hue, Vietnam, which I ate in eighteen minutes and then sat in front of the empty bowl for another five minutes before I was capable of standing up.

It was, and I mean this, the greatest soup encounter of my life. And I have lived a full life. I have eaten soup in thirty-one countries. This bowl.

The Rest of Vietnam, In Soup

I cannot do justice to everything in one piece. But a brief survey of the other significant bowls:

Bún riêu, Hanoi, 6am: A tomato and crab broth that I did not know existed before this trip and now consider essential to human experience. Score: 9.0. The tofu was the surprise — crisped at the edges, soft in the center, absorbing the broth. I ate this at an outdoor table on a plastic stool on a Hanoi morning with motorbikes going past and considered whether there was a version of my life in which I moved here specifically for this soup. Score: 9.0. The life change: still under consideration.

Bánh canh, Hội An, lunch: A thick udon-adjacent noodle in a clear broth with shrimp and pork — a soup that is confident in its simplicity, that doesn't need to be anything more than what it is. Score: 8.7. I ate it twice. The second time I sat next to a German couple who were discussing their travel insurance and I ate my soup and felt quietly superior, which is not a feeling journalism is supposed to produce but occasionally does anyway.

The Top Five Soups, Ranked

  1. Bún bò Huế, unnamed side-street shop, Hue — 9.8
  2. Pho bo, Old Quarter institution, Hanoi — 9.3 (Bowl #7)
  3. Pho bo, Tây Hồ family shop, Hanoi — 9.1 (Bowl #14)
  4. Bún riêu, street vendor, Hanoi Old Quarter, 6am — 9.0
  5. Bánh canh, Hội An street stall — 8.7

The full spreadsheet covers 47 bowls across 11 days, four cities, and one overnight train where the dining car served a soup I would generously classify as 6.4 and unkindly classify as broth that had once been in the same room as a chicken.

This trip made me a better person. Or at least a better soup person. Whether those are the same thing is something I am still working out, and I think I'll be working it out for a while, probably over a bowl of something warm at a plastic table somewhere in a city that does this better than anywhere else in the world.

— Paul "Mambo Taxi" Largent, Vietnam. The bún bò Huế shop does not have an address I can give you. That is not me being precious. I genuinely found it by following someone. Go to Hue. Follow someone carrying lunch. That's the whole methodology.
