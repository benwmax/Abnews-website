Seema Spends Three Days In Dildo, Newfoundland And Files A 4,000-Word Report

The residents of Dildo, Newfoundland (population: 1,200) have "heard every single joke, multiple times, from journalists who could not maintain eye contact." This correspondent attempted to maintain eye contact. She mostly succeeded.
📰
Seema Krishnamurthy
Senior Anchor & Field Correspondent  |  Filed from Dildo, Newfoundland 🇨🇦  |  June 5, 2025  |  9 min read
🐟
The harbour at Dildo, Newfoundland. This is not a place that needs your commentary. Photo: S. Krishnamurthy / AlphaBro News
Dildo, Newfoundland: a working fishing community on Trinity Bay. The town has a squid interpretation centre, a museum, and an extremely small gift shop that has been sold out of commemorative items since approximately 2018.  |  AlphaBro News

I want to begin this piece by noting that I did not choose this assignment. This is not, strictly speaking, true — I accepted it when offered, which is a form of choosing — but I want it on the record that I understood, when I booked the flight to St. John's and then the rental car along the Trans-Canada and then the 90-minute drive down Route 80 toward Trinity Bay, exactly what I was doing and why. I was going to a town called Dildo for professional reasons. My editor is Paul. Paul thinks this is funny. Paul is not wrong, but that is between Paul and me.

The town of Dildo sits on the western shore of Dildo Arm, a narrow bay off Trinity Bay on the Avalon Peninsula of Newfoundland. Population: approximately 1,200. Founded in the early eighteenth century. The origin of the name is, genuinely, disputed — possible derivations include the Portuguese word "doudo" (foolish), the Spanish "dil de agua" (a navigational term), and a local theory involving a man named Jeremy Dildo that I found in a visitor pamphlet and cannot independently verify. The pamphlet did not cite sources. I have tried to find records of a Jeremy Dildo. I have found nothing. I have come to believe he may not be real, but I include him because the pamphlet was professionally printed and someone made a decision about that.

Arrival

I arrived on a Tuesday afternoon. The drive from St. John's is pleasant in the way that all drives in Newfoundland are pleasant: rugged coastline, sparse trees bent sideways by decades of Atlantic wind, the ocean appearing and disappearing between low hills. Route 80 narrows as you approach town and the first sign appears without ceremony: DILDO. Standard Canadian municipal signage, white on green. I photographed it once. I decided this was enough.

The town is tidier than I expected, which is to say I had braced for a place made self-conscious by its name and found instead a working community that is simply getting on with things. There are houses, a church, a school, a Tim Hortons 20 minutes up the road in Whitbourne, and, crucially, the Dildo Interpretation Centre, which is about fisheries history. The fisheries history of Dildo is, it turns out, genuinely interesting. The area has sustained fishing communities for centuries. The squid has been economically central to the region. This is the thing the Dildo Interpretation Centre is about, and it is a good interpretation centre, and I say that as someone who has been to a lot of interpretation centres in the pursuit of stories that should have been about something else.

The Gift Shop Situation

The gift shop at the Dildo Interpretation Centre has been sold out of commemorative mugs since 2018. This is not an exaggeration. The woman working at the desk, whose name I am declining to include because she has dealt with enough, told me they cannot keep anything in stock. "We order more," she said, "and they're gone in a week. People come from everywhere." She said this without visible pride or distress. She said it the way you say something that is simply true.

I asked what else they stocked. She showed me some postcards. They had postcards. I bought three. She thanked me in a tone that suggested she did not find the transaction particularly meaningful, which was fair.

The gift shop also had a small selection of local history books. I bought one. It is 340 pages and covers the fishing industry of Trinity Bay from 1700 to 1985. I have read 80 pages of it on the plane home. It is very good.

The Locals

I have developed a working theory, after three days in Dildo, that the residents of the town have sorted themselves into three groups: those who have made peace with the situation and occasionally find it funny, those who are actively tired of the situation and would prefer not to discuss it, and those who have simply decided it is not a situation at all and are confused by the question.

I interviewed residents across all three groups. I am not going to quote any of them directly because I promised I wouldn't and also because they didn't say anything particularly quotable — they said ordinary things about living in a small town on the coast of Newfoundland, which is to say things about fish, about winters, about their children moving to Halifax or Toronto, about the ferry to Bell Island, about whether the Tim Hortons in Whitbourne is actually better than the one in Carbonear. Normal things. This is a normal town. The name is funny to people who are not from here. To people who are from here, it is just where they live.

One man, a retired fisherman I will call Gerald because that was his name and he said I could use it, told me that the worst year was 2015, when a YouTube video about the town got two million views and "every week there were people with cameras." He paused. "You're here with a camera," he said. I acknowledged this. "You seem nice enough," he said. This was the most generous assessment of my presence I received during the trip, and I appreciated it.

The Fish

I ate fish and chips at a diner on the main road, served in a paper basket lined with wax paper, the cod fresh enough that I stopped and looked at it for a moment before eating. Salt cod has been the economic and cultural backbone of Newfoundland for four centuries. The cod fishery collapse in 1992 devastated communities across the province. What you are eating, when you eat fish and chips in a small Newfoundland town, is connected to all of that — to history and economics and community survival and grief — in a way that makes the food mean something beyond the food itself.

It was also just really good fish and chips. Some things can be more than one thing.

The Museum

The Dildo and Area Museum occupies a small building near the harbour and contains exactly what you would expect: photographs, artifacts, logbooks, tools of the fishing trade, records of families who have lived in this area for generations. I spent ninety minutes there. The volunteer docent, a woman in her seventies whose family has been in Dildo since the 1840s, walked me through the exhibits with the patience of someone who has done this many times and finds the material genuinely worth discussing.

We talked about the cod moratorium, about the transition to crab and shrimp, about the outport depopulation that has hollowed out dozens of Newfoundland communities since the 1950s. Dildo has held on. "We've always held on," she said. "We're stubborn." She did not bring up the name once. Neither did I, for the first time in three days, and it was a relief.

A Final Note

I drove back to St. John's on Thursday morning. I had filed, at that point, approximately 3,800 words of notes and draft copy, which I would later reduce to 4,000 words in the final edit because it turns out there was more to say than I expected, which is something that happens sometimes when you go somewhere with low expectations and pay attention.

Dildo, Newfoundland is a real town with real people and a real history that is far more interesting than its name. It does not need journalists visiting it for the reasons journalists typically visit it. It deserves better than that. It deserves, at minimum, to be taken seriously by the people who show up with cameras and then go home and write the word "Dildo" fourteen times in the headline.

I am one of those people. I am aware of this. I have no good defense except that I also bought the history book and I am reading it on my own time, which is maybe not enough but is something.

Dildo, Newfoundland: a functioning community, good fish, a gift shop that has been emptied by people like me, and more than enough history to justify a serious return visit by someone who does better work than I did here. I am sorry, Dildo. You were very patient.

— Seema Krishnamurthy, reporting from Dildo, Newfoundland, Canada
