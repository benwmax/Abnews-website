Paul Attempts To Eat All Of Bangkok In One Day. Hospitalized On Day Two.

Paul Largent had a spreadsheet, a schedule, and thirty-seven pages of notes on Bangkok's street food scene. He made it to 40 dishes. Then, at midnight, he made one final decision: a durian shake. He describes this, in retrospect, as "a mistake."
🕵️‍♂️
Paul "Mambo Taxi" Largent
Lead Anchor & Chief Investigative Correspondent  |  Filed from BNH Hospital, Bangkok 🇹🇭  |  May 30, 2025  |  11 min read

Editor's note: this piece was filed from BNH Hospital, Bangkok, where Mr. Largent is "resting comfortably" and has already asked about the wifi password and whether the kitchen does a breakfast menu. He is fine. The piece has been lightly edited. — S. Krishnamurthy

I'm going to tell you about the durian shake in a moment. First let me tell you about the spreadsheet, because the spreadsheet was a masterwork and I want it acknowledged before we get to the part where everything went wrong.

The spreadsheet had 67 items: Bangkok street food, organized by neighborhood, sub-organized by category (noodles, rice dishes, grilled items, soups, drinks, sweets), with estimated time per dish, travel time between locations, and a color-coded priority ranking. Green: essential. Yellow: if time allows. Red: stretch goals. The durian shake was in red. I am a man who respects his own color-coding system. I had one job.

The Day: A Timeline

7:00am — Dish 1: Pad Thai, Thipsamai, Phra Nakhon. The most famous pad thai in Bangkok, possibly the world. I arrived when they opened. The noodle texture was precise, the egg perfectly cooked, the dried shrimp exactly right. Score: 9.1. Strong start. Everything was possible.

9:00am — Dishes 4–6: Khao Tom (rice soup), satay, and a grilled pork skewer from Yaowarat Road. The khao tom was gentler than expected — I rated it 7.8, mentally noted it as a palate reset. The satay was 8.5. The grilled pork skewer was 9.0 and made me briefly reconsider my career in journalism in favor of a career in standing near this grill.

11:00am — Dishes 10–14: Chatuchak Weekend Market sweep. Mango sticky rice (9.2 — the mango was extraordinary), papaya salad, fried chicken with basil, coconut ice cream, and something labeled in Thai that I pointed at hopefully and which turned out to be a crispy omelet over rice that was 8.8 and I still think about.

1:00pm — Dishes 15–19: Chinatown dim sum and roast duck. The roast duck from T&K Seafood was 9.3. I rated it 9.3 on the spot and I stand by this. The skin was lacquered, the meat was—look, if you are reading this from a hospital bed, the memory of excellent roast duck is both a comfort and a complication. The dim sum was fine. Roast duck is still the story.

3:00pm — Dishes 20–25: Som tam competition. This is where I made my first error in judgment. I found a som tam vendor in Silom who made three different regional variations, and I decided, rather than choosing one, to order all three and do a comparative analysis. This was scientifically sound. Calorically, it was a choice. I ate all three. Average score: 8.7. It took forty-five minutes. I was twenty-five dishes in at 4pm. I was on schedule. I was also, if I'm being fully honest with the reader, starting to feel the beginning of what I would later describe as "the situation."

5:00pm — Dishes 26–31: Sukhumvit street sweep. Boat noodles (8.9), grilled corn (7.4 — respectable but not remarkable), a bowl of guay teow that I ate while standing over a storm drain because there was nowhere to sit, which I gave an 8.1 for flavor and a situational deduction for the ambiance. Tom yum from a cart outside a 7-Eleven: 8.7. A mango smoothie I had as a palate cleanse: 9.0 — the best single mango smoothie I have ever consumed, and I have consumed many.

7:00pm — Dishes 32–37: Dinner run, Chinatown. I was thirty-two dishes in and still functional. This is important. I was tired but functional. The body was flagging — not staging a protest, just expressing a firm preference for horizontal surfaces. I ate six more things. A crab omelette (9.1), stir-fried morning glory (8.3), a whole fish I pointed at in a tank (9.4 — the best piece of fish I ate in Bangkok), and three other things I can no longer separately recall, which is probably a sign.

10:00pm — Dishes 38–39: Night market, Khao San area. By 10pm, my body was, and I want to be precise about this, staging a firm but fair protest. Not an emergency. A professional grievance. I ate two more dishes — a spring roll (8.0) and a banana pancake from a street cart (8.6) — and sat on a plastic stool under a fan and had a cold Chang beer and felt, genuinely, proud of myself. Thirty-nine dishes. I was going to make it. I just needed to execute the final entry on the color-coded spreadsheet.

Midnight — Dish 40: Durian shake.

I'm going to tell you what a durian shake is, in case you don't know. Durian is a Southeast Asian fruit with a complex, custard-like interior and an exterior that smells, famously, like something that a different, less adventurous journalist would describe as "challenging." The shake is durian blended with ice and condensed milk. It is very sweet. It is very cold. It is very durian. It was, at midnight, after thirty-nine dishes, on a body that had been consuming food for seventeen straight hours and was, at this point, operating purely on stubbornness and residual journalism school training, not a good decision.

I drank the whole thing. I gave it 7.1. It was fine.

It was, in retrospect, not fine.

BNH Hospital: A Review

I checked myself in at 12:47am. The staff were exceptional. The intake process was efficient. My doctor, a man named Dr. Chaiwat who spoke excellent English and had clearly seen people in my situation before, explained the situation in calm clinical terms and recommended rest, fluids, and "perhaps fewer than forty food items tomorrow." I asked if he meant as a general recommendation or specifically for tomorrow. He looked at me. I did not ask the follow-up question.

The hospital room is comfortable. The breakfast is: rice congee with ginger, which is an 8.4 and extremely appropriate given the circumstances. I have been awake since 6am thinking about the roast duck.

BNH Hospital, Bangkok: 8.1 on the Mambo Taxi scale. The service is extraordinary, the food is therapeutic, and the wifi is good enough to file this piece. Points deducted for the reason I am here. Points awarded for Dr. Chaiwat's bedside manner and the fact that he did not lecture me. He didn't need to. I am doing that myself.

I will try again. Not immediately. But there are twenty-seven items left on the spreadsheet, and the roast duck vendor opens at noon.

— Paul "Mambo Taxi" Largent, BNH Hospital, Bangkok. Day 2. The durian shake was a mistake. The other thirty-nine things were not. I regret nothing except the durian shake. I may try the durian shake again in a few months.
