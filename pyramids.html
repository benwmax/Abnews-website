Seema Investigates Whether The Pyramids Are Just Big Triangles. A Deep Dive.

After consulting zero Egyptologists, one patient camel guide named Mohammed, and the geometry of the situation, Krishnamurthy's verdict is: "Technically yes. But also no. It's extremely complicated and I feel bad for asking."
📰
Seema Krishnamurthy
Senior Anchor & Only Adult In The Room  |  Filed from Giza, Egypt 🇪🇬  |  May 31, 2025  |  10 min read
🏔️
The Great Pyramid of Giza. It is, structurally speaking, more complex than a triangle. Photo: S. Krishnamurthy / AlphaBro News
The Pyramid of Khufu, Giza Plateau, Egypt. Height: 138.5 meters. Base: 230.33 meters per side. Sides: four triangular faces meeting at an apex. Whether this constitutes "just a big triangle" is, as Krishnamurthy reports, "the wrong question, but an interesting one."  |  AlphaBro News

I am going to explain how this assignment happened, because I think context is important.

Paul Largent was expensing a camel ride in Egypt — two camel rides, actually, which is a separate story I have written separately — and while reviewing his receipts, I mentioned that I was also in Cairo on other business. Paul said, and I am quoting this verbatim from a voice message he sent at 1:47am: "seema you should investigate the pyramids. are they just big triangles. like structurally. this is what journalism is for."

I told him that the pyramids are among the most studied structures in human history and that this was an absurd research question. He sent a thumbs-up emoji, which I interpreted as agreement. He did not mean it as agreement. He meant it as encouragement.

I went to Giza. I stood in front of the pyramids. I tried to answer the question.

The Research Question

Structurally speaking: are the Pyramids of Giza just big triangles that kept going?

This requires immediate refinement. A triangle is a two-dimensional shape. The pyramids are three-dimensional structures. What we are actually asking is whether they are square pyramids — a three-dimensional shape with a square base and four triangular faces meeting at an apex — and whether the characterization "just big triangles" accurately captures this.

I requested an interview with the site's resident Egyptologist for comment on this question. The Egyptologist was unavailable. I was told, with evident patience, that the site receives approximately 14,000 visitors per day and that the Egyptologist had "other priorities." I accepted this. It seemed fair.

The Camel Guide's Perspective

My camel guide was named Mohammed. He has been guiding tourists at the Giza plateau for seventeen years. He has heard many questions about the pyramids. When I explained my research question — are they, structurally, just triangles that kept going? — he looked at me for a moment with the expression of a man who has decided that this is, at minimum, not the strangest thing he has been asked today.

"They are not triangles," he said. "They have a square base."

"So they're square pyramids."

"Yes."

"Which is technically a three-dimensional extension of a triangle."

Mohammed considered this. "Technically," he said, "but that is not the interesting thing about them."

I asked him what was.

He gestured at the Great Pyramid of Khufu — 138 meters tall, 2.3 million stone blocks, the only surviving Wonder of the Ancient World — and said, simply: "That they exist."

I wrote that down. It felt like the most useful thing anyone would say to me that day, and I was correct.

The Geometry

For the sake of rigor, I should document what I observed. The Great Pyramid of Khufu has: a square base (230.33 meters per side), four triangular faces (isosceles triangles, approximately 51.8-degree slope angle), and a single apex. It is, therefore, a square pyramid, which is geometrically classified as a quadrilateral pyramid, which is a subset of the broader category of pyramidal shapes.

A triangle has three sides. The pyramid's faces are triangles. The base is a square. The overall shape is what a mathematician would call a square pyramid and what Paul "Mambo Taxi" Largent would call "a big triangle" and what both of them would be technically partially correct about.

Paul expensed two camels, by the way. I rode one camel. When I asked about the second camel on his expense report, he said it was "for context." I have noted this in our accounting system as "disputed." I have also written a separate piece about it, which Paul has asked me not to publish. I am publishing it.

The Camel Itself

The camel's name was also Mohammed (this is common, Mohammed told me; there are many Mohammeds at Giza, both among the guides and among the camels). Camel Mohammed was approximately seven years old, extremely large, and expressed clear opinions about the pace at which I wanted to travel. His opinion was: slower. We compromised.

I rode Camel Mohammed in a broad arc around the base of the Great Pyramid. I looked at it from the side, from the corner, from close up and from far away. From every angle, it is triangles meeting a square base. From every angle, it is also genuinely astonishing — not because it is triangles, but because it is 138 meters tall and made of 2.3 million blocks and it has been standing here for 4,500 years and it will almost certainly outlast everything I have ever written by approximately 4,500 years.

I felt a little bad for the question. Not bad enough to not file the piece. But bad.

Conclusion

Are the Pyramids of Giza just big triangles that kept going?

Technically yes, in the sense that their faces are triangles and their overall shape is a triangular projection extended into three dimensions from a square base. Technically no, in the sense that this description misses approximately everything important about them — the engineering, the scale, the history, the labor, the 4,500 years, the fact that they are the only surviving Wonder of the Ancient World and I am sitting on a camel asking if they're triangles.

Mohammed (the guide, not the camel) was right. The interesting thing is that they exist. The rest is geometry.

I am sorry for the question. I am publishing the piece. These two things can both be true.

— Seema Krishnamurthy, filed from Giza. The separate piece about the two camels follows. Paul asked me to note that the second camel "enriched the experience." I am noting it. I am not endorsing it.
