Seema Attends A Cricket Match In Mumbai, Understands Nothing, Files 2,800 Words

Krishnamurthy's sweeping account of a five-day Test match at Wankhede Stadium, Mumbai, contains approximately zero cricket analysis, a growing friendship with a retired banker named Rajan, and 800 words about the samosas. She defends the samosa wordcount. The defense holds.
📰
Seema Krishnamurthy
Senior Anchor & Only Adult In The Room  |  Filed from Wankhede Stadium, Mumbai 🇮🇳  |  May 27, 2025  |  13 min read
🏟️
Wankhede Stadium, Mumbai, Day 2. The crowd noise was audible from two streets away. Photo: S. Krishnamurthy / AlphaBro News
Wankhede Stadium, Mumbai, Maharashtra, India. Capacity: 33,108. Cricket knowledge of Seema Krishnamurthy at point of entry: zero. Cricket knowledge at end of Day 5: partial, but growing.  |  AlphaBro News

I want to address the samosas first, because I know they're going to be the issue.

Eight hundred words on the samosas. Yes. Eight hundred words. I have been asked, by Paul, by our nominal editor (the concept), and by at least three people who read an advance version, whether eight hundred words on the samosas is too many words on the samosas. My answer: the samosas at Wankhede Stadium, from a vendor named Vinay who has been selling samosas at this ground for twenty-two years, are not a side note. They are a primary source. I am standing by the wordcount.

Now, the cricket.

Day 1: What Is Happening

I arrived at Wankhede Stadium at 9:30am for a Test match between India and England. I had done preliminary research. I understood: two teams, eleven players each, bats and balls, wickets involved, the whole thing takes five days, which is a structural choice I find either visionary or deeply eccentric depending on my mood.

What I did not understand, and would spend five days coming to grips with: the LBW rule. I am going to return to this. It is not resolved by the end of this piece. I want to be clear about that upfront.

The man in the next seat was named Rajan. He was a retired banker from Pune, in his late sixties, at the cricket with his son, who had immediately moved several rows away to sit with friends. Rajan was therefore available to answer questions. When I introduced myself as a journalist from AlphaBro News, he said "ah" in a tone that suggested he had heard of AlphaBro News, or possibly that he was simply being polite about not having heard of it. Either way he offered me a samosa from Vinay's cart and we were friends from that moment.

"The batting side tries to score runs," Rajan explained, as the first ball was bowled. "The fielding side tries to get them out."

"How do you get them out?"

Rajan listed six or seven ways. I wrote them all down. I understood four of them immediately. The fifth I partially understood. The sixth — LBW — I did not understand and have still not understood to any degree of real confidence. When I asked Rajan to explain it more than once, he explained it more than once, with great patience, and I nodded in a way that I hoped was convincing, and then an LBW decision happened on the field and I asked "was that the LBW?" and Rajan said "yes" and I said "I see" and I did not fully see.

The Samosas (A Brief Summary)

Vinay's samosas are: roughly triangular (the pyramid piece has made me conscious of this framing), filled with spiced potato and peas, fried to a crispness that achieves a structural integrity — you can hold it with one hand while writing notes with the other, which was essential — and served with a green chutney that Vinay applied from a squeeze bottle with the speed and precision of a man who has done this approximately ten thousand times.

I ate fourteen samosas over five days. I want to be clear that I did not eat fourteen samosas in one day. I ate between two and four per day, which is a professional quantity and I will not be questioned on this. The average score: 9.1. The peak was Day 3, afternoon session, when Vinay gave me what I am convinced was a freshly fried batch — the pastry had a quality that earlier and later ones lacked. Day 3 samosa, afternoon: 9.4.

Eight hundred words. They earned it.

Days 2-4: Things I Learned

Over four days of patient explanation from Rajan, I learned: the difference between a Test match and other cricket formats (length, primarily, but also something about the Test match's particular philosophy — it rewards endurance, concentration, the long game in a way that Rajan described as "civilization," which seemed right); how runs are scored; what a maiden over is; why certain fielding positions have names that I cannot type here without AlphaBro News publishing them as a headline; and the general shape of the game, which I began to feel, around Day 3, as a rhythm rather than a series of inexplicable events.

The crowd was extraordinary. Wankhede at full noise — when India took a wicket, or when a particular player came to bat — produced a sound that I cannot fully describe except to say that it is sound with mass. It presses on you. When India took a key wicket on Day 4 and the stadium went up, I went up with it, which surprised me, and I looked at Rajan, and Rajan was already standing, and I thought: I understand something now that I did not understand five days ago. I'm not sure what it is. But it's something.

Day 5: The LBW Rule and an Emotional Conclusion

I did not fully understand the LBW rule by Day 5. I understood its broad principle: the ball would have hit the wicket, but instead hit the batsman's leg, and so the batsman is out as if the leg had been the wicket. What I did not understand: how anyone can determine, with confidence, that the ball "would have" hit the wicket. This involves geometry and projection and something called ball-tracking technology for reviews, and I asked Rajan about it several times, and each time he explained it, and each time I said "right, of course," and I still had the distinct feeling of a woman nodding at a concept she has decided to table indefinitely.

India lost two wickets in the final session. I was upset about this in a way I did not anticipate being upset. I was invested. I had spent five days in this stadium with this crowd and this man explaining things to me and fourteen samosas and I cared how it came out. India held on for a draw, which Rajan described as "a very good result," and the crowd agreed, and I agreed, and we shook hands, Rajan and I, and he said "good luck with your piece" and I said "thank you for the cricket education" and he said "you still don't understand LBW" and I said "no, I don't" and he laughed and went to find his son.

Two thousand eight hundred words. Eight hundred about samosas. I stand behind all of it.

— Seema Krishnamurthy, filed from Wankhede Stadium, Mumbai. To Rajan: if you are reading this, the samosa score is accurate and I have been thinking about Day 3, afternoon session ever since.
