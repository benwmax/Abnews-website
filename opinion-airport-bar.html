Every City Should Have An Airport Bar On Airside. This Is Not Negotiable.

You've cleared security. Your gate is forty minutes away. You have a roll-aboard, a bruised ego from the body scanner, and three hours until boarding. You deserve a drink. Paul Largent makes the case.
🕵️‍♂️
Paul "Mambo Taxi" Largent
Lead Anchor & Chief Investigative Correspondent  |  Opinion  |  June 4, 2025  |  5 min read
🍹
An airport bar. Not the good one. But a bar nonetheless. Photo: P. Largent / AlphaBro News
An airport bar somewhere in the world. Paul Largent has been to 38 of them and has opinions about all of them. This is one of the better ones.  |  AlphaBro News

I am going to make this simple, because the proposition is simple, and I am tired of people complicating it.

Every city should have an airport bar on the airside — that is to say, past security, where the gates are, where the people who are actually flying sit and wait. Every city. Not "most cities." Not "larger hubs." Every city with a commercial airport should have, past the security checkpoint, a bar where a person can get a drink that is not terrible at a price that is not criminal, and sit in it, and be a person who is in an airport for a legitimate reason, which is to say they have a flight to catch and they would like something to hold while they wait for it.

This is not a controversial position. I am going to explain why people act like it's a controversial position, and then I am going to explain why they are wrong.

The "It's 8am" Objection

The first objection I always get is: "Paul, it's 8am." This objection reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of the airport as a space.

Time zones do not apply inside airports. This is not me being cute about it — this is a structural reality of air travel. If you are at an airport at 8am, you may have woken up at 3am to catch this flight, in which case it is effectively 5pm in your body. You may be catching a red-eye and 8am is actually the end of your night. You may have come from a country where it is already 4pm and your body knows this. You may simply be someone who is about to sit in a metal tube for eleven hours and would like one drink first.

The airport exists outside of local time. That is the point of the airport. It is a liminal space where normal rules are suspended, where you are between where you were and where you're going, where the only obligation is to be at the right gate at the right time. In this space, it is always 5pm somewhere you just left. The bar is appropriate. This is not negotiable.

The Counter-Evidence (Addressed)

Some airports argue that they serve the community around them and should not be places of drinking. To this I say: the community around an airport is not in the airport. The people in the airport are travelers, and travelers have already accepted that they are about to be inside a pressurized aluminum tube at 35,000 feet with recycled air and a meal in a foil tray, and they deserve a drink before that.

Some airports argue that alcohol leads to problems on aircraft. To this I say: bad airport bars lead to people drinking badly in bad airport bars. A good airport bar — well-lit, comfortable, with competent bartenders and an appropriate selection — is an environment that promotes responsible, moderate drinking by adults who are about to spend several hours not drinking. It is a civilizing influence. The Nairobi airport bar has incredible peanuts. Nobody causes problems after the Nairobi airport bar peanuts. This is science.

The Ideal Bar

What I am asking for is not complicated. A bar. Past security. With: natural light, if possible. A cocktail list that includes something that is not just beer and white wine. Snacks that are not a plastic-wrapped muffin from a rack. A bartender who is making drinks and not simultaneously running a café, a newsstand, and a charging station. Comfortable stools. Maybe a window with a view of the tarmac, which is not glamorous but is at least something to look at.

That is it. That is the bar. It can exist. It does exist, in Nairobi and Tokyo and Bangkok and Singapore and several other places where someone has understood that the airport bar is a service, not a concession. It can exist everywhere. I am asking for everywhere.

This is my position. It has not changed in three years of airport bar research. It will not change. The bar should be there. You have cleared security. You deserve it.

— Paul "Mambo Taxi" Largent. Filed from an airport. As usual.
