Seema's Tokyo Ramen Bracket: 32 Bowls, One Winner, No Regrets

Four days. Thirty-two bowls. A fully seeded tournament bracket with judging criteria. The winner — a counter shop in Shinjuku open until 4am with six seats — "made me consider never leaving Japan," Krishnamurthy reports.
📰
Seema Krishnamurthy
Senior Anchor & Only Adult In The Room  |  Filed from Tokyo, Japan 🇯🇵  |  June 2, 2025  |  12 min read
🍜
Bowl #27 of 32. Shinjuku, 2am. The light was bad. The broth was extraordinary. Photo: S. Krishnamurthy / AlphaBro News
Bowl number twenty-seven. Shinjuku, Tokyo, approximately 2am on day three. Krishnamurthy reports she ate it standing up because there were no seats available. She scored it 8.9. It did not make the final four.  |  AlphaBro News

I want to be upfront about something: this was, by any objective measure, a frivolous assignment. Paul suggested it with his characteristic non-specificity — "tokyo ramen deep dive seema, lots of bowls, very scientific" — and because I had two weeks in Japan already planned for other coverage, I agreed, and then I spent the following seventy-two hours designing what I believe is the most rigorous ramen evaluation framework ever constructed by a non-Japanese journalist with no culinary training.

I stand behind it. The methodology was sound. The winner deserved to win. The brackets do not lie.

The Methodology

Thirty-two bowls, seeded by advance research: Tokyo ramen rankings, local food blogs, two English-language guides, and recommendations from the editor of a food publication who owed me a favor. Seedings were assigned without tasting — this is essential for bracket integrity.

Each bowl was judged on five criteria, scored 1-10:

  • Broth depth — complexity, richness, whether it had clearly been simmered for a number of hours I am unwilling to calculate
  • Noodle texture — firmness, chew, appropriateness to the style
  • Toppings — quality, integration, the chashu specifically (always the chashu)
  • Atmosphere — the room, the light, the noise, the sense that you are in exactly the right place at exactly the right time
  • Value — what you paid for what you received, in the context of where you were eating it

I ate eight bowls per day for four days. I acknowledge this was aggressive. My body handled it with what I would describe as "cautious skepticism" by day two and "resigned professionalism" by day four. I have no complaints. I would do it again. This is a sentence I did not expect to write.

Notable Contestants

Bowl #4 (Shibuya, 11am, famous queue shop, 3-hour wait): I waited three hours. I would like that on the record. The broth was tonkotsu — dense, almost architectural, the kind of broth that feels like a decision rather than a flavor. Score: 8.7 composite. The queue reduced its atmosphere score; I had been standing for three hours and could not fully access wonder.

Bowl #11 (Nerima, family shop, no English menu): A quiet place in a residential neighborhood. The owner's mother was visible through the kitchen window. The broth was shoyu — clear, precise, with a depth that suggested it had been made by someone who had been making it for a very long time. The noodles were slightly thick, perfectly chewy. Score: 8.8. I came back the next day for lunch, which I acknowledge is not within the rules of a single-elimination bracket. I am calling it "supplemental research."

Bowl #19 (Shinjuku, 2am, six-seat counter): At 2am on day three, I was at a six-seat counter in a Shinjuku alley, eating a bowl of miso ramen that I can only describe as warm geometry — every element precise, everything in its place, the fat pooled at the surface in a pattern that seemed designed. I gave it a 9.1. It did not make the final four because it lost, narrowly, to the bowl that would eventually win. I think about this outcome.

The Final Four

The final four: Bowl #4 (Shibuya tonkotsu), Bowl #11 (Nerima shoyu), Bowl #27 (2am Shinjuku miso), and Bowl #31 (Shinjuku 4am counter, miso-shio hybrid). The semifinal matchups were extremely close. Bowl #11 lost to Bowl #31 by 0.3 composite points, which I found genuinely distressing.

The final was Bowl #27 versus Bowl #31. Both shops are within a ten-minute walk of each other. I ate them back to back on the final morning. I took notes. I scored them. I sat outside on a concrete step at 5am and looked at my spreadsheet and felt, somewhat unexpectedly, the specific satisfaction of a job done exactly as planned.

The Winner

Bowl #31. A counter shop on a Shinjuku side street, open from 11pm to 4am, six seats, no signage in English, a laminated picture menu with four options. The owner is a man of approximately sixty who does not speak and does not need to. The broth is a miso-shio hybrid — lighter than pure miso, more complex than shio — that I have not been able to fully articulate in the week since I ate it. The noodles are thin. The chashu is two slices, no more, which is the correct number of slices. There is half a soft-boiled egg. The atmosphere at 3am is precisely the atmosphere of a place that has been exactly what it is for many years and has no interest in being anything else.

Final score: 9.4 composite. Highest of the bracket.

I sat at the counter and ate it slowly, which is not how I had been eating ramen for four days — speed had been necessary, schedule had been paramount. I sat at that counter at 3am and ate it slowly and thought: this is the best bowl of ramen I have ever eaten, and I have just eaten thirty-one other bowls to prove it, and that is an absurd way to have arrived at this moment, and I do not regret a single one of them.

I left Tokyo at 7am. I nearly didn't. I went back to the shop at 4am to confirm the score, which — fine, yes — was outside the bracket rules. The score held. The bowl was, if anything, better the second time.

I have the spreadsheet. I am not sharing it. It is mine.

— Seema Krishnamurthy, Tokyo. The Nerima family shop deserved a better semifinal draw. I am sorry, Bowl #11. You were very good.
